About the Institution

One of the world's oldest and most comprehensive museums

Founded in 1753, the British Museum holds a permanent collection of around 8 million objects — among the largest and most comprehensive in existence — representing human history, culture and art from across the globe and throughout time.

The museum's Department of Ancient Egypt and Sudan oversees one of the world's leading collections of Egyptian antiquities, with over 100,000 objects spanning more than three millennia of pharaonic history, from the pre-Dynastic period through to the Roman era.

Book of the Dead of Ani · c.1275 BCE

The ancient Egyptians were profoundly concerned with what happened after death. They believed the soul — the ka and ba — could continue to exist in the afterlife, provided the body was preserved and the correct rituals were performed. The Book of the Dead was a collection of magical spells written on papyrus scrolls and placed in tombs to guide the deceased through the underworld.

Ani's copy, created around 1275 BCE, is considered the most illustrated and among the most beautiful examples in existence. At nearly 24 metres long, it charts the soul's dangerous journey through the Duat — the realm of the dead — and the critical weighing of the heart against the feather of Ma'at, goddess of truth.

The 70-Day Process

1

Removal of Organs

The brain was removed via the nostrils. The liver, lungs, stomach, and intestines were stored in canopic jars.

2

Natron Drying

The body was packed in natron salt for 40 days to draw out all moisture and prevent decay.

3

Wrapping

Hundreds of metres of linen were used, with protective amulets placed between each layer.

4

The Opening of the Mouth

A final ritual ceremony to restore the mummy's senses and allow the soul to use the body in the afterlife.

Gallery Four

3,000 Years of Civilisation

c.3100 BCE

Unification of Egypt

King Narmer unites Upper and Lower Egypt, founding the First Dynasty. The double crown — the pschent — becomes the symbol of unified rule. Writing, hieroglyphs, and monumental architecture emerge almost simultaneously.

c.2560 BCE

Great Pyramid of Giza

The Great Pyramid, built for Pharaoh Khufu, stands 146 metres tall and is constructed from over 2.3 million stone blocks. It remains the oldest of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World and the only one still largely intact.

c.1353 BCE

The Amarna Revolution

Pharaoh Akhenaten abandons the traditional pantheon and institutes worship of the Aten (sun disc) alone — the world's first known monotheistic state religion. His queen, Nefertiti, plays an unprecedented role in state and religious affairs.

c.1279 BCE

Reign of Ramesses II

The longest-ruling and most celebrated pharaoh, Ramesses the Great reigns for 66 years, commissioning more temples and statues than any other ruler. His mummy is one of the best-preserved ever discovered.

30 BCE

Fall of the Ptolemaic Kingdom

After the death of Cleopatra VII and the suicide of Mark Antony, Egypt becomes a province of Rome. Three thousand years of pharaonic civilisation come to an end, though Egyptian religious practices persist for centuries more.

Gallery Five — Feature Object

The Rosetta Stone

Discovered in 1799 near the town of Rosetta (Rashid) in northern Egypt by soldiers in Napoleon's expedition, the Rosetta Stone is a granodiorite stele inscribed with three versions of a royal decree issued in 196 BCE during the reign of Ptolemy V. The three scripts — Ancient Egyptian hieroglyphics, Demotic script, and Ancient Greek — allowed linguists to decode hieroglyphics for the first time in over 1,400 years.

The stone was transferred to British custody after the French surrender to Britain in Egypt in 1801. It has been on public display at the British Museum since 1802 and is the most visited object in the collection.
